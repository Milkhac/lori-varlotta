Ensuring a seamless and strategic transition of leadership—the final and most delicate act of a presidency.

Timing and Shifting Dynamics

The Handoff Phase begins the moment your departure is announced and continues until your successor is in place.

Once your exit is public, organizational dynamics shift dramatically. Some constituents will try to pressure you to shelve unpopular decisions for the next administration, while others who believe they have your ear may seize the moment to push their personal priorities forward. Your final challenge is balancing these hot-button issues (that you should resolve) against major initiatives (best left for your successor to own and shape).

Common Activities for the Outgoing Leader

Institutionally loyal and future-focused leaders use this phase to strategically wrap up their tenure:

  • Finalize projects that are budget-savvy and benefit the institution.
  • Resolve outstanding issues that could otherwise burden a successor.
  • Transfer critical knowledge, process know-how, and confidential relationship context to the new leader.
  • Fast-track necessary but unpopular operational changes (like budget cuts).
  • Defer major long-term strategic shifts to leave space for the new leader's vision.

A Real-Life Example of a Successful Handoff

One outgoing president spent their final year consolidating six academic schools into three interdisciplinary ones.

This decisive move eliminated three deanships, reduced administrative "bloat," and redirected resources toward coveted new teaching opportunities. Crucially, this restructuring provided a clear framework upon which the incoming president could immediately build. The structure encouraged interdisciplinary learning and team teaching, which was well received by students and faculty—a significant win for institutional continuity.

Conclusion: Leadership Through Impact

The presidency is often messy, nonlinear, and unpredictable. Longevity isn’t the only measure of success; some universities need a steady leader, while others require a disruptor to reset systems for the next leader to refine.

What matters most in the end is not how long you sat in the chair, but whether you used the phases you had to move the institution forward in ways that truly mattered.

Transition Essentials

  • Avoid: Major capital campaign commitments or permanent cabinet-level hires.

  • Do: Create a well-sequenced transition communication plan with the Board.

  • Manage: Increased internal jockeying and the potential for staff anxiety over job security.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should a university president focus on after announcing their departure?

The most pressing focus is the transition communication plan with the trustees. Prioritize finalizing essential projects and resolving lingering issues. It is also smart to craft guidance documents or reflections (e.g., annotated climate surveys, donor briefings) for the successor's benefit.

How can an outgoing president set up their successor for success?

A departing leader offers information only when asked by the incoming leader (taking a "back seat"). The goal is to clear pathways by sharing institutional knowledge and candid insights, not to shape the successor’s agenda. Restraint is key to respecting the new president's authority.

Which decisions should be left for the incoming president to make?

Strategic, long-term decisions that lock the institution into a direction, such as launching a new degree program, committing to a capital campaign, or making permanent cabinet-level hires, should be deferred. The incoming president deserves the space to set the vision and agenda.

What makes a transition successful?

Successful transitions require two conditions: the departing president shows grace in handing off the torch, and the incoming president shows respect (privately and publicly) for prior efforts and achievements.
