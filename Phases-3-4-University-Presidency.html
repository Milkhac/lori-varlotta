Center Stage and Tough Decisions

Phases 3 & 4 of a University Presidency

The shift from establishing authority to confronting high-stakes challenges, where accountability is at its peak.

3

The President: Delivering Results

Timing: Typically begins after the first full academic year and extends for several years. The novelty has worn off.

No longer “new,” the president now embodies the administration. Expectations shift to measurable outcomes: follow-through on strategic intentions, effective problem-solving, and relationship-building both on and off campus. The president's philosophy is now translated into concrete practices across governance, enrollment, and community engagement.

Key Activities

Designing and executing the strategic plan and multi-year budget scenarios.

Restructuring shared governance to ensure all constituents have meaningful input.

Implementing structural or personnel adjustments (e.g., merging departments or reassigning VP portfolios).

Example: The Value of Foresight

When COVID-19 hit in 2020, presidents with strong multi-year financial planning and collaborative boards fared better than those forced into immediate, reactive cuts.

The "Damn" President: Tough Decisions

4

Timing: Can emerge anytime after the honeymoon, often accelerated by crises or unforeseen challenges.

Here, the leader confronts choices that test their resolve and define their tenure. Early goodwill has faded, easy wins are behind, and scrutiny is constant. Decisions can range from addressing structural deficits and reallocating resources to navigating high-profile legal or ethical complexities. This is the moment when the "damned if you do, damned if you don't" reality of leadership hits hardest.

Key Activities

Addressing structural deficits or responding quickly to significant market shifts.

Closing under-enrolled programs, reducing faculty lines, or reallocating resources to high-impact areas.

Handling high-profile legal, compliance, or ethical matters with broad visibility.

Example: Balancing Governance

A president who worked to include staff and student voices in governance while preserving faculty authority satisfied some groups but inevitably alienated others.

The Defining Test of Leadership

In these center-stage phases, university presidents operate under intense scrutiny, balancing competing demands while advancing the institution’s mission. The ability to make difficult choices with transparency, inclusivity, and strategic foresight is what truly distinguishes successful leadership in higher education.

Reference: The Demands of Center Stage: Delivering Results and Making Tough Decisions by Lori Varlotta

Frequently Asked Questions

What changes after the honeymoon phase ends?

Expectations shift from promise to performance. Presidents must deliver concrete results in governance, finance, enrollment, and community engagement while clarifying the roles of key players in the administration.

Why is multi-year budget planning critical?

Single-year budgeting is too risky for tuition-dependent institutions. Scenario-based multi-year plans clarify future revenue and expenses, enabling better, proactive decision-making under uncertainty.

How are unpopular decisions navigated?

Presidents weigh short-term backlash against long-term institutional health, combining transparent rationale, empathy for those affected, and incremental progress. The goal is long-term benefit, even if it means short-term dissatisfaction.

What defines a “damned if you do, damned if you don’t” scenario?

These are moments when any action will provoke both approval and criticism. The true test of leadership is not universal agreement but whether the decision is ethically sound, transparently communicated, and demonstrably in the institution’s long-term best interest.
