A University President’s Basic Outline to Budgeting and Resource Allocation

A principled guide for higher education leaders on balancing competing interests, ensuring mission alignment, and building long-term financial sustainability.

Strategic Principles

Embrace the "Both/And"

Leaders must simultaneously brake and accelerate, navigating complex choices and setting clear expectations for unavoidable tradeoffs.

Three Guiding Ends

Every decision must advance: Mission-Alignment, Long-term Sustainability, and Student Success.

Uphold Fiduciary Duty

Act in the institution's overall best interests (care, loyalty, obedience) rather than individual departmental aspirations.

Proactive Transparency

Communicate the financial context early and often, creating constructive space for necessary conflict.

A. The Both/Ands of Resource Allocation

  • Brake and accelerate simultaneously
  • Rely on both the ethical and legal imperatives of a fiduciary
  • Set expectations for hard and necessary tradeoffs

Budgeting involves navigating "both/and" realities, like demanding speed while requiring restraint, or balancing innovation with tradition. The president's fiduciary duty requires balancing legal and ethical imperatives with the institutional mission. Hard tradeoffs are unavoidable—expanding one program may require downsizing another. Leaders must model candor and courage, setting the expectation that budget conversations will be difficult but necessary, ensuring principled resource allocations even if not uniformly satisfactory.

B. The Three “Ends” of Resource Allocation

Mission-Alignment, Long-term Institutional Sustainability, and Student Success

  • Reinforce institutional viability over departmental preferences
  • Align strategic priorities with resource realities
  • Take some calculated risks with contingency plans ready to go

Every budget decision must advance at least one of these three guiding ends. Financial stewardship means prioritizing long-term institutional viability and student learning over immediate pressures or departmental preferences. By using these ends as a touchstone, leaders gain clarity, allow for measured risk, and demonstrate that difficult tradeoffs serve the institution's higher purpose.

C. The Principles and Processes of Budgeting

Performance-based allocation principles

  • Make data-driven decisions
  • Reward individuals and departments that are constructive
  • Decide which financially unstable programs will be sustained as “loss leaders” and which will be paused or sunsetted

Transparent decision-making processes

  • Provide criteria and rationale early and clearly
  • Engage constituents with appropriate experience and expertise
  • Share and document final decisions

The process is as vital as the outcome. A data-driven, student-centric performance-based allocation framework ensures resources support programs with strong outcomes (recruitment, retention). Transparency is critical: clearly communicate criteria and rationalize decisions while engaging appropriate stakeholders. Leaders must be cooperative without ceding their ultimate decision-making authority.

D. The Philosophies That Undergird Resource Allocation

“Fair” resource allocation

  • Define what “fair” means in this context
  • Be clear: in resource allocation, like in DEIJ, “fair” is not the same as “equal.”
  • Consider some quantitative models for “fair” distribution

The legal philosophy of fiduciary duty

  • Underscore duties of care, loyalty, and obedience
  • Act in the institution’s overall best interests rather than the interests of a few departments

Budgeting is shaped by fairness and fiduciary duty. "Fair" allocation means prioritizing mission and potential return—it does not mean equal across-the-board cuts or investments. The fiduciary framework mandates that presidents adhere to duties of care, loyalty, and obedience, always placing institutional sustainability above the interests or aspirations of individual departments.

E. Individual Interests vs. Institutional Wellbeing

Faculty and staff interests vs. institutional wellbeing

  • Build trust through honest communication
  • Manage to disappointment while maintaining engagement
  • Acknowledge the sacrifice needed at times

Protection of student programs and services is paramount

  • Set and achieve ambitious retention and graduation rates
  • Allocate resources with the above in mind
  • Focus more on long-term sustainability than short-term wins

When budgets tighten, cutting employee benefits is often more defensible than eliminating essential student programs. Leaders must communicate honestly that tradeoffs are between “bad” and “worse.” While internal airing of disappointment is constructive, externalizing conflict (via press or public channels) can damage enrollment and lead to deeper cuts.

F. The Communication and Engagement during Resource Allocation

Proactive and transparent approach

  • Expose the context in which the decisions are embedded
  • Communicate early and often about potential changes
  • Solicit feedback before decision is made and provide updates once it is finalized

A constructive place for conflict

  • Recognize not hide conflict
  • Create space for respectful disagreement
  • Establish and enforce “civil” engagement rules

Silence breeds distrust. Leaders must take a proactive and transparent approach, exposing the financial context early and inviting structured feedback *before* decisions are finalized. Conflict is inevitable, but it can be managed productively by creating discussion opportunities with ground rules to demonstrate respect and engender trust, even when painful outcomes are unavoidable.

G. Shared Governance and Data Utilization

Develop a Budget or Resource Allocation Process that leverages Shared Governance

  • Clear governance structures and decision authority
  • Advisory committees vs. decision-making bodies
  • Balancing consultation with timely action

Evidence-based resource allocation models

  • Activity-based costing and performance metrics
  • Regular budget reviews and adjustments
  • Hybrid models combining traditional and performance-based funding

Shared governance works best with clear decision authority (advisory vs. decision-making bodies) to prevent gridlock and false expectations. Presidents should establish budget advisory councils to provide input. Data-driven models (like performance metrics or hybrid funding) strengthen the process by grounding conversations in facts, making resource allocation about impact rather than politics, and preserving stakeholder trust.

H. Conclusion: Leadership Through Complexity

Resource allocation is the president's most complex responsibility, demanding courage, resilience, and wisdom. Principled leadership balances fairness with fiduciary duty, reinforcing the mission and building community. Addressing inevitable tensions head-on helps foster understanding across different perspectives.
