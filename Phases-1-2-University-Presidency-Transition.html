The Transition Phases of a University Presidency

Phases 1 & 2: From Preparation to Presence

The journey begins with intense, private learning, followed by the highly visible "honeymoon" period focused on engagement and small, impactful wins.

Phase 1

President-Elect

"I Don’t Know You; You Don’t Know Me"

Timing: From announcement until the official start date. Operates entirely behind the scenes, without formal authority.

Key Preparatory Activities

Analyzing Institutional Documents: Budgets, accreditation reports, debt summaries, and climate surveys.

Reviewing Public Resources: Websites, alumni publications, social media, and news posts.

Compiling a list of Questions and Observations to guide future conversations in office.

Rule of Engagement

All external engagement must be coordinated with the sitting president or board chair to prevent overstepping formal authority.

Phase 2

New President

"Getting to Know You, Getting to Know Me"

Timing: Typically spans the first 12–18 months in office—the "honeymoon period" of goodwill and mutual curiosity.

Key Engagement & Impact Activities

Embarking on a Learning and Listening Tour across all constituent groups and locations.

Implementing small, visible initiatives (early wins) to signal impact and commitment.

Assessing the senior leadership team for alignment with institutional goals and readiness for transformation.

Example: The Possibility Fund

A president launched a “Possibility Fund” offering small grants for high-impact, low-cost projects, demonstrating an early commitment to innovation and engagement.

Laying the Foundation for Success

These transition phases are crucial. They dictate the pace and direction of the rest of the presidency. Phase 1 provides the essential knowledge base needed to ask the right questions, and Phase 2 provides the platform to build the trust required for later, tougher decisions.

Reference: Phase 1 & Phase 2 of a University Presidency: Transition by Lori Varlotta

Frequently Asked Questions

What should a president-elect focus on before taking office?

Listen, learn, and build institutional awareness by reviewing documents and media. Crucially, avoid making any official decisions prematurely, as you lack formal authority.

What is expected in the first 90 days?

The focus should be on presence, engagement, and listening rather than major changes. The goal is to set a tone of respect, commitment, and readiness for future action.

How can an outgoing president-elect meet faculty and staff before officially starting?

Yes, but only if coordinated with the sitting president. Early visits are designed for storytelling, listening, and initial rapport-building, not policy discussion.

How can trust be built with the campus community during the honeymoon?

Trust is built through consistent presence, reliable follow-through on small promises, genuine empathy, and transparent, personal engagement.
